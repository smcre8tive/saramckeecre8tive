IncluDesign Immersive Projector Game & Learning Ecosystem

Multi-product ecosystem for design students builds empathy through a visual-imparment simulaiton immersive projector game.

Role: Product Designer | Team: 3 Designers | Duration: 3 weeks | Tools: Figma, Figjam, Canva

Detail related to this project hero image

Challenge & Problem

During a SCAD graduate level class in 2024, I had the opportunity to design an immersive environment product with a two other graduate team-mates. We decided to focus on accessible education design concerns for students.

Gen-Z design students value accessible design but lack engaging ways to learn how to apply it effectively.

How might we help Gen-Z design students learn accessible design in engaging ways with immersive environment technology?

Research & Discovery

To help clarify user need, Inclusive Design definitions, expectations and market value, we used Figma & Figjam to gather supporting secondary research and market data to support our product direction.

After using Microsoft's Inclusive Design support kit for another project, I suggested we use it's definition and principles to support our process and my team-mates agreed. We also decided to use the Microsoft Persona Spectrum to help empathize with humans with diverse levels of impairment.

Microsoft Inclusive Design's methodology is born out of digital environments, that enables and draws on the full range of human diversity. Most importantly, this means including and learning from people with a range of perspectives.

During our seondary research, we learned that:

90% of global corporations report being committed to diversity and inclusion efforts in 2024. Only 4% of these companies state having a disability inclusion focus.
62% of Gen-Z consumers have a strong values base and prefer to buy from brands that care about important social issues, including inclusion & diversity.

We also conducted conducted primary research with a SCAD design students using a Google survey and interviewed a graduate school inclusion design director to gather some important pain-points & insights that guided our next steps.

44% of design students rated their inclusive and accessibility design knowledge at a 2/5 emphasizing a gap in practical application.

Walker, SCAD’s Inclusion Director, highlighted a global challenge: measuring inclusion and belonging. She highlighted that the national reading level standards that are grade 7 and content should reflect this. She also emphasized the importance of making experiences accessible & involving underrepresented communities in the design process, saying:

“When I think of inclusion, I think of family. How do we ensure every family member is involved in the process of fulfilling or engaging in an action in the most purposeful way to achieve our goal?”
-Director Walker

Our research identified four key user needs:

  • Empathy-building: Students need immersive ways to understand accessibility challenges.
  • Practical application: There's a gap between theory and hands-on inclusive design.
  • Accessible tools: Learners require easy-to-use resources to apply inclusive principles.
  • Measurable outcomes: Educators want ways to track progress in accessibility learning.

We decided on the following features goals:

  • Gamification & Empathy: Empathy and gamification as the drivers for each feature to ideally engage university students in learning.
  • Accessible Learning: Understand and apply accessibility standards and related tools to our design and workflow.
  • Accessible tools: Learners require easy-to-use, and finacially accessible resources to apply inclusive principles.
  • Measurable outcomes: Learners want ways to track progress in accessibility learning with application of learning and reward.

Ideation & Iteration

Low-fidelity sketches and wire-frames helped us ideate through multiple versions of our problem solution.

Our initial sketches included ideas for a responsive web-app learning game with AR envorinment scanner and an immersive projector game.
Starting first with the web-app with AR scanner, we made a pivot to the interactive projector on a university campus when user feedback highlighed a need for more a immersive experience. We integrated the web-app into an inclusive design ecosystem where the app would act as a support tool.
We researched interactive projector experiences & games and decided on a game to support empathy for visual imparement. This user-flow identifies the parts of our game and how the user would transition from one screen to the next.
For our low-fidelity wire-frames, we used the Figma Apple Oculus' design system as a base for our projector simulation prototype.

Visual Design

We each took on different tasks to help break up the high-fidelity design work. I focused on the first two parts of the game while my other team-mates took on the design system and the final game screens. Here are the design contributions that I made to the final product.

Inspired by other larger format screen interface designs, I created an intro screen with a login sequence for students. I identified an opportunity for our team to use our web-app designs as a controler and accessibility support tool. We added a hand holding a matching mobile screen to each prototype screen to help simulate the full ecosystem experience.
To help increase engagement, I explored Figma's prototype animation features to help bring the experience to life with a simulation game spinner.

I used a visual from the Sight Center of Northwest Ohio to help prototype a visual imparement simulation environemnt quiz. Future goals could be centered on adding more immersive video with sound.

Prototyping & Testing

We worked together remotely to combine our designs into a high-fidelity interactive prototype in Figma to simulate the projector game experience. We used Canva to design and present our process & prototype with SCAD design students and faculty for feedback and usability.

One of our team-members used Audocad to create a few architectural renderings of an environment with our projector game in use by students.
These are some of the slides from our presentation and represent project work done by my team-mates.

Class feedback highlighted the engaging nature of the game but also pointed out areas for improvement, such as clarification on the ecosystem and more diverse scenarios for simulaitons. We ideated future goals for edits to this after this feeedback session including:

  • Adding more immersive video and sound to enhance the simulation experience.
  • Incorporating a wider range of visual impairments and scenarios to broaden empathy-building.
  • Testing the prototype with diverse human users that vary on the accessibility needs spectrum.
  • Finding an interactive projector to use for real-world game design testing.

Results & Impact

This project was completed in a 3-week academic quarter and was well-received by both peers and faculty. The interactive projector game concept demonstrated a novel approach to teaching inclusive design principles through immersive technology.

Key outcomes included:

  • Empathy through Experience: The immersive nature of the projector game effectively fostered empathy among design students.
  • Engagement in Learning: Gamification elements increased student engagement and motivation to learn about accessibility.
  • Future Potential: The project highlighted the potential for immersive technologies in design education, suggesting avenues for further exploration and development.

Overall, the IncluDesign Interactive Projector game represents a promising step towards more inclusive and engaging design education, aligning with the values and needs of Gen-Z design students in 2024. I learned so much working remotely with this team related to team-work, inclusive design, interactive projectors, interactive affordances and signifiers and creative prototype animation simulation.
