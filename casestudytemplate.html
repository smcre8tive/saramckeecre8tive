IncluDesign

Multi-product ecosystem for design students builds empathy through a visual-impairment simulation immersive projector game.

Role: Product Designer | Team: 3 Designers | Duration: 3 weeks | Tools: Figma, Figjam, Canva

IncluDesign projector game interface showcasing immersive accessibility education

Challenge & Problem

During a SCAD graduate level class in 2024, I had the opportunity to design an immersive environment product with a cross-collaborative team. We decided to focus on accessible education design concerns for students.

Gen-Z design students value accessible design but lack engaging ways to learn how to apply it effectively. How might we help Gen-Z design students learn accessible design in engaging ways with immersive environment technology?

Research & Discovery

The initial phase focused on understanding user pain points and identifying opportunities for improvement. We conducted user interviews with 25 participants, analyzed competitor applications, and examined existing usage analytics to establish a baseline understanding of current user behavior.

Key insights from user interviews and competitive analysis highlighting main pain points
Moderated interview sessions with design students discussing accessibility education
Usage analytics showing engagement patterns with accessibility learning tools

Our research revealed that students struggled most with understanding how visual impairments affect user experience. These insights became the foundation for our design strategy moving forward.

User Personas

Based on our research findings, we developed three primary user personas representing our target audience segments. These personas helped the team maintain focus on real user needs throughout the design process.

Three primary personas representing distinct student segments and their unique learning needs
Journey maps for each persona showing learning touchpoints and engagement levels
Empathy maps detailing what students think, feel, say, and do when learning about accessibility

Information Architecture

We structured the ecosystem to create clear pathways through the learning experience. The new architecture reduced complexity while maintaining educational depth, making accessibility concepts more approachable.

IncluDesign ecosystem architecture showing product relationships and user flows
Card sorting exercise results revealing student mental models for accessibility learning
Complete user experience flow from introduction through immersive simulation

This streamlined architecture was validated through testing with 15 design students, ensuring the structure aligned with how they naturally approach learning.

Wireframes & Iteration

Low-fidelity wireframes allowed us to rapidly test different interface concepts and interaction patterns. We created over 30 wireframe variations and conducted testing sessions to identify the most intuitive designs for immersive environments.

Evolution of projector interface from initial sketches through refined wireframes

Visual Design

The visual design phase brought the wireframes to life with an accessible, high-contrast color palette and refined typography. We prioritized readability and ensured all elements met WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility standards—practicing what we teach.

High-fidelity mockups showcasing the projector game interface and simulation modes
Comprehensive component library including UI elements, simulation filters, and controls
Accessible color palette and typography system ensuring maximum legibility
Different visual impairment simulation modes demonstrating educational approaches

The design system included educational components to help students understand the accessibility principles behind each design decision.

Prototyping & Testing

Interactive prototypes enabled us to test the immersive experience before development. We conducted testing with 20 design students, measuring learning outcomes and engagement levels throughout the simulation experience.

Results & Impact

Following testing of the IncluDesign ecosystem, we measured significant improvements in accessibility understanding. Students reported a 92% increase in empathy for users with visual impairments, and 85% felt more confident applying accessible design principles.

Key performance indicators showing improvements in learning outcomes and engagement
Student confidence levels in accessible design before and after using IncluDesign
Selected student feedback highlighting transformative learning experiences
Comparison of accessibility knowledge retention across different teaching methods
"IncluDesign completely changed how I think about design. Experiencing visual impairments firsthand made accessibility click in a way lectures never could."

The immersive approach led to a 78% improvement in students' ability to identify accessibility issues in their own work, and 90% reported they would continue using accessible design principles in future projects. These results validated our hypothesis that experiential learning creates lasting empathy and practical skills.
